\begin{abstract}
Shadow removal is a critical pre-processing step for document digitization, yet a fundamental question remains unresolved: \textit{what is the optimal way to integrate shadow prior information into a Transformer-based restoration network?}
While prior work has proposed various fusion mechanisms, each making different architectural choices without systematic justification, the field lacks a rigorous understanding of \textbf{when and why} different fusion strategies succeed or fail.
In this paper, we conduct the first systematic diagnosis of five shadow fusion strategies---concatenation, cross-attention, FiLM modulation, sigmoid-gated fusion (SGGF), and model scaling---under strictly controlled conditions using a Restormer backbone.
Our study reveals a surprising \textbf{synthetic-real dichotomy}: FiLM modulation achieves the best performance on synthetic data (+1.34~dB over baseline, 24.77~dB PSNR on SD7K) yet catastrophically collapses on real-world data (NaN at epoch~125--130 in two independent runs) due to $\tanh$ gradient saturation.
Conversely, simple concatenation---the most lightweight approach---emerges as the best cross-domain generalizer (18.59~dB SD7K$\rightarrow$RDD, outperforming all sophisticated alternatives).
We further identify a striking \textbf{asymmetric domain gap}: models trained on RDD transfer to SD7K at only $\sim$14.6~dB, while the reverse direction achieves $\sim$18.6~dB, revealing fundamentally different levels of domain transferability.
Based on these diagnostic insights, we derive \textbf{SGGF}, a sigmoid-gated fusion module that avoids $\tanh$ saturation while matching FiLM's expressiveness, achieving the best TextPSNR (27.75~dB) and OCR recovery (100.2\%) with stable training across both domains.
Our GatedLarge variant surpasses the prior state-of-the-art DocDeshadower by +2.96~dB on SD7K.
These findings establish evidence-based principles for multi-modal fusion in document restoration and caution against evaluating fusion strategies on synthetic benchmarks alone.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================================

Document image shadow removal is a fundamental pre-processing step in document digitization pipelines.
Shadows cast by hands, cameras, or page curvature degrade visual quality and severely impair downstream tasks such as OCR, layout analysis, and document understanding.
Unlike natural shadow removal, document shadow removal requires preserving fine-grained text structures while eliminating large-scale illumination variations.

The dominant paradigm in modern document shadow removal follows a two-stream architecture: a \textit{shadow encoder} extracts features from an auxiliary shadow map (or grayscale input), and a \textit{fusion module} injects these features into the restoration backbone.
With the rise of Transformer-based backbones like Restormer~\cite{zamir2022restormer}, a natural question arises: \textbf{which fusion mechanism works best for integrating shadow priors into a Transformer?}

A survey of recent literature reveals a fragmented landscape with no clear consensus.
BEDSR-Net~\cite{lin2020document} employs a global feature modulation approach via adaptive instance normalization.
DocDeshadower~\cite{li2023single} adopts squeeze-and-excitation channel attention (SGCA) for cross-modal re-weighting within a CNN encoder-decoder.
Other works use concatenation~\cite{Liuspectral}, attention-based aggregation, or simple element-wise operations---each making different architectural choices without a systematic comparison.
\textbf{The community lacks an evidence-based answer to a fundamental question:} when does a sophisticated fusion mechanism (cross-attention, FiLM) outperform simple concatenation, and under what conditions does it break?

To answer this question, we conduct a controlled diagnostic study of five representative fusion strategies within an identical Restormer backbone (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Concatenation}: Channel-wise concatenation of shadow and decoder features, followed by a reduction convolution. The simplest baseline.
  \item \textbf{Cross-Attention (SGCF)}: Decoder features as queries, shadow features as keys/values, with multi-head cross-attention and zero-initialized residual gate.
  \item \textbf{FiLM Modulation (SGFM)}: Shadow features predict per-channel scale and shift parameters, modulating decoder features via $\text{feat} \times (1 + \tanh(\gamma)) + \beta$.
  \item \textbf{Gated Fusion (SGGF, proposed)}: A sigmoid-gated convex combination derived from our diagnostic findings, avoiding $\tanh$ saturation.
  \item \textbf{Model Scaling (Large)}: A wider variant (dim=64) with concatenation fusion, testing whether ``more capacity + simple fusion'' matches ``less capacity + complex fusion.''
\end{enumerate}

All models are trained under identical conditions (200 epochs, same optimizer, data augmentation, and loss function) on both synthetic (SD7K) and real-world (RDD) datasets, with cross-dataset generalization evaluated in both directions.

Our study yields three key findings that go beyond a simple performance ranking:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Finding 1 — The synthetic-real dichotomy.} FiLM achieves the best PSNR on synthetic SD7K (24.77~dB, +1.34~dB over baseline) yet catastrophically collapses on real-world RDD (NaN at epoch~125--130 in two independent runs). We trace this to $\tanh$ gradient saturation: RDD's diverse shadow patterns push modulation parameters into the saturation regime, causing vanishing gradients from which the optimizer cannot recover. Gradient clipping rescues training (SGFM~v2 achieves 37.01~dB on RDD), revealing that the issue is \textit{optimization dynamics}, not model capacity.

  \item \textbf{Finding 2 — The asymmetric domain gap.} Cross-dataset evaluation reveals a striking asymmetry: models trained on RDD and evaluated on SD7K achieve only $\sim$14.6~dB PSNR (near-random), while SD7K-trained models reach $\sim$18.6~dB on RDD. Furthermore, \textit{simple concatenation generalizes best} (18.59~dB SD7K$\rightarrow$RDD), outperforming all sophisticated fusion mechanisms. Larger models (dim=64) generalize \textit{worse} than their dim=48 counterparts, suggesting that increased capacity exacerbates synthetic-domain overfitting.

  \item \textbf{Finding 3 — Evidence-based design of SGGF.} Motivated by the above findings---FiLM excels when stable but suffers from $\tanh$ saturation; cross-attention is redundant given Transformer self-attention; concatenation is robust but lacks adaptivity---we derive SGGF, a sigmoid-gated fusion module. SGGF uses a bounded [0,1] gate with smooth gradients, achieving convex-combination numerical stability. It achieves the highest TextPSNR (27.75~dB) and best OCR recovery (100.2\%) while matching the efficiency of the simplest baselines (+0.3M parameters, $<$1ms overhead).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Our contributions} form a three-level pyramid:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textbf{C1.}] \textbf{The first systematic diagnosis} of Transformer shadow fusion strategies, revealing \textit{why} different mechanisms succeed or fail through controlled experiments, training dynamics analysis, and cross-domain evaluation.
  \item[\textbf{C2.}] \textbf{Discovery of two fundamental phenomena}---the synthetic-real optimization dichotomy and the asymmetric domain gap---that challenge the common practice of evaluating fusion strategies on synthetic benchmarks alone.
  \item[\textbf{C3.}] \textbf{SGGF}, a gated fusion module directly derived from diagnostic evidence, achieving state-of-the-art TextPSNR and OCR recovery with minimal overhead, surpassing DocDeshadower by +2.96~dB on SD7K.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Document Shadow Removal}

Early document shadow removal methods relied on physics-based models with hand-crafted priors~\cite{bako2016minimal,Liuspectral}, assuming simplified illumination models (e.g., uniform background, smooth shadow boundaries).
These approaches struggle with complex real-world shadows involving non-uniform illumination, colored backgrounds, and irregular shapes.

CNN-based methods introduced learned feature representations for shadow removal.
BEDSR-Net~\cite{lin2020document} uses a two-stage coarse-to-fine framework with background color estimation and global feature modulation---an early form of shadow-guided fusion that globally adjusts encoder features.
DocDeshadower~\cite{li2023single} employs a U-Net with squeeze-and-excitation channel attention (SGCA) for cross-modal re-weighting: shadow features are pooled and used to re-weight document feature channels.
However, their SGCA module was designed for CNN backbones; our experiments reveal that it becomes \textit{harmful} on Transformers (-1.67~dB), as Transformer self-attention already performs cross-modal interaction, making additional channel re-weighting redundant and disruptive.

Recently, Transformer architectures have shown promise in document restoration by capturing long-range dependencies.
Yet, to our knowledge, no prior work has systematically examined how to optimally integrate shadow priors into Transformer backbones---a gap this study directly addresses.

\subsection{Fusion Strategies in Multi-Modal Restoration}

Multi-modal fusion has been studied across various restoration tasks, but existing work typically proposes a \textit{single} mechanism without comparing alternatives.
We organize prior fusion choices by their underlying mechanism:

\textbf{Concatenation-based fusion} is used in numerous works for its simplicity and robustness.
In document shadow removal, Liu et al.~\cite{Liuspectral} concatenate shadow maps with input images at the network entrance.
In video restoration, EDVR~\cite{wang2019edvr} concatenates aligned multi-frame features.
A key insight from our study is that concatenation's simplicity is a feature, not a limitation: it introduces no activation nonlinearity that could saturate, no attention weights that could overfit, and no modulation parameters that could destabilize training.
This explains its superior cross-domain generalization (18.59~dB SD7K$\rightarrow$RDD, best among all methods).

\textbf{Attention-based fusion} uses cross-attention to enable content-dependent aggregation from auxiliary modalities.
In image restoration, Restormer~\cite{zamir2022restormer} uses cross-attention for multi-frame feature fusion; in document shadow removal, the idea is to let decoder features ``query'' shadow information.
However, our experiments reveal a critical finding: \textbf{cross-attention underperforms on Transformers} (+0.02~dB over concatenation on SD7K).
We attribute this to the Transformer decoder already encoding cross-modal context through self-attention over skip connections, making explicit cross-attention largely redundant while introducing significant memory overhead (4$\times$ VRAM at equivalent resolution).

\textbf{Modulation-based fusion} (FiLM~\cite{perez2018film}) uses predicted scale/shift parameters for lightweight, spatially-uniform modulation.
It has been successfully applied in style transfer, visual reasoning, and conditional image generation.
Our study is the first to reveal its \textbf{optimization fragility} on real-world data: $\tanh$ saturation causes vanishing gradients at epochs 125--130, but gradient clipping fully rescues training (SGFM~v2: 37.01~dB on RDD).
This finding re-contextualizes FiLM: it is not inherently unstable; rather, it requires careful gradient management on diverse real-world data.

\textbf{Gating-based fusion} uses sigmoid-activated gates to selectively blend features.
SENet~\cite{hu2018squeeze} popularized channel-wise gating for single-modal feature re-weighting; GLU~\cite{shazeer2020glu} variants gate linear units in language models.
Our SGGF adapts gating for \textit{cross-modal} fusion: the gate is computed from \textit{both} modalities, enabling context-dependent blending rather than fixed re-weighting.
The sigmoid [0,1] range and convex combination guarantee numerical stability---a deliberate design choice motivated by FiLM's $\tanh$ saturation failure.

\subsection{Why Restormer as the Backbone?}

We select Restormer~\cite{zamir2022restormer} as our backbone for three reasons: (1) it is a widely-adopted strong baseline in image restoration, making our findings relevant to a broad audience; (2) its U-Net-style multi-scale encoder-decoder with skip connections provides a natural testbed for multi-scale fusion at different resolutions; (3) its Transformer design lets us investigate whether self-attention already handles cross-modal interaction, a key question our study answers in the affirmative.
By keeping the backbone fixed, we \textbf{isolate the effect of the fusion strategy}, enabling the first apples-to-apples comparison in this domain.

\section{Proposed Method}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Overall Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

Our framework, illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}, consists of three components:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Shadow Encoder}: A lightweight CNN that extracts multi-scale shadow features $\{s_1, s_2, s_3\}$ from the grayscale/shadow-map input at $\frac{1}{4}$, $\frac{1}{8}$, and $\frac{1}{16}$ resolution.
  \item \textbf{Restormer Backbone}: A 4-level encoder-decoder Transformer with multi-head self-attention and gated feed-forward networks. The encoder processes the input document image; the decoder reconstructs the shadow-free output.
  \item \textbf{Fusion Module}: Injects shadow features into the decoder at levels 2 (dim$\times$2) and 3 (dim$\times$4). We experiment with five variants (Sec.~\ref{sec:fusion}).
\end{enumerate}

The network is trained end-to-end with a combined loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_1 + \lambda_{\text{ssim}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSIM}} + \lambda_{\text{fft}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{FFT}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_1$ is the pixel-wise L1 loss, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSIM}}$ is the structural similarity loss, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{FFT}}$ is a frequency-domain loss that penalizes spectral differences.

\subsection{Shadow-Guided Fusion Strategies}
\label{sec:fusion}

Let $\mathbf{F}_d \in \mathbb{R}^{C_d \times H \times W}$ denote decoder features and $\mathbf{F}_s \in \mathbb{R}^{C_s \times H \times W}$ denote shadow encoder features at the same spatial resolution.
We define five fusion operators below.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Concatenation (No SGCA)}

The simplest baseline. Shadow features are projected to match decoder channels via a $1\times1$ convolution, then concatenated channel-wise and reduced back:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}_{\text{out}} = \text{Conv}_{1\times1}\big([\,\mathbf{F}_d \,;\, \text{Proj}(\mathbf{F}_s)\,]\big)
\end{equation}
This is parameter-free beyond the reduction convolution and serves as our ``shadow-guided but no attention'' baseline. The term ``No~SGCA'' indicates that we intentionally disable the Squeeze-and-Excitation channel attention used in prior work~\cite{li2023single}.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{SGCF: Shadow-Guided Cross-Attention Fusion}

Decoder features serve as queries $\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{W}_q(\mathbf{F}_d)$, while shadow features provide keys and values $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{W}_k(\mathbf{F}_s)$, $\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{W}_v(\mathbf{F}_s)$.
Multi-head cross-attention (4 heads) produces:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}_{\text{attn}} = \text{Softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}
\end{equation}
The output is combined via a learnable zero-initialized gate $\gamma$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}_{\text{out}} = \mathbf{F}_d + \gamma \cdot \mathbf{W}_o(\mathbf{F}_{\text{attn}})
\end{equation}
Initializing $\gamma=0$ ensures the network initially behaves as the vanilla decoder before gradually incorporating shadow information.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{SGFM: Shadow-Guided Feature Modulation (FiLM)}

A lightweight alternative where the shadow features predict per-channel modulation parameters:
\begin{equation}
  \gamma(\mathbf{F}_s),\; \beta(\mathbf{F}_s) = \text{Split}\big(\text{GAP}(\mathbf{F}_s) \rightarrow \text{FC} \rightarrow \text{FC}\big)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}_{\text{out}} = \mathbf{F}_d \odot \big(1 + \tanh(\gamma)\big) \;+\; \beta
\end{equation}
The $\tanh$ bounds the scale factor in $(0, 2)$, but its saturation at $\pm1$ can cause vanishing gradients when the network needs large modulation values.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{SGGF: Shadow-Guided Gated Fusion (Proposed)}

To address FiLM's gradient saturation, we propose a sigmoid-gated fusion module.
Shadow features are first projected to match decoder channels:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{S} = \text{Conv}_{1\times1}(\mathbf{F}_s)
\end{equation}
A channel-wise gate is computed from the concatenated features via global average pooling:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g} = \sigma\big(\text{FC}_2(\text{ReLU}(\text{FC}_1(\text{GAP}([\mathbf{F}_d; \mathbf{S}]))))\big) \in \mathbb{R}^{C_d \times 1 \times 1}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, and FC$_1$/FC$_2$ form a bottleneck with reduction ratio 4.
The final output is a convex combination gated by $\mathbf{g}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}_{\text{out}} = \mathbf{F}_d \odot \mathbf{g} \;+\; \mathbf{S} \odot (1 - \mathbf{g})
\end{equation}

Key properties: (1) Sigmoid gates are bounded in $(0,1)$, preventing gradient explosion; (2) The convex combination ensures numerical stability since the output is always a weighted average of two bounded inputs; (3) The module adds minimal parameters ($\sim$0.1M at dim=48) since the FC bottleneck compresses to $C_d/4$ channels.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Model Scaling (Large)}

Instead of modifying the fusion operator, we scale the Restormer backbone width from dim=48 to dim=64.
This increases capacity uniformly across all components (encoder, decoder, shadow encoder, fusion).
We pair scaling with the simplest fusion strategy (concatenation) to test whether ``more capacity + simple fusion'' can match ``less capacity + sophisticated fusion.''

\section{Experiments}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Datasets and Implementation Details}

\textbf{SD7K} (Shadow Document 7K)~\cite{li2023single} is a synthetic dataset containing 6,720 training and 190 test image pairs at 320$\times$320 resolution.
Shadow maps are generated procedurally; ground truth is the clean document without shadows.

\textbf{RDD} (Real Document Dataset) is a real-world dataset with 1,080 training pairs at variable resolutions.
We train at 256$\times$256 random crops and validate on the held-out test set.

\textbf{Training protocol.} All models are trained for 200 epochs using AdamW ($\beta_1=0.9$, $\beta_2=0.999$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) with initial learning rate $2\times10^{-4}$ reduced by half at epochs 100 and 150.
Batch size is 1 per GPU; gradient accumulation of 2 is used where memory permits.
All experiments run on 4$\times$NVIDIA RTX 3090 (24GB).

\textbf{Evaluation metrics.} We report PSNR, SSIM, RMSE, and TextPSNR (PSNR computed exclusively on text regions identified by a pre-trained text detector).

\textbf{Key observations:}

\textit{(1) Shadow guidance consistently helps.} Every shadow-guided variant outperforms the vanilla Restormer, confirming that shadow priors are beneficial for document restoration. Even the simplest concatenation (No SGCA) yields +0.31~dB PSNR.

\textit{(2) FiLM leads on synthetic data but is unstable.} SGFM achieves the best PSNR (24.77~dB) and SSIM (0.9308) on SD7K, representing a +1.34~dB improvement over the baseline. However, on RDD (see below), FiLM failed twice due to NaN gradients at epochs 130 and 125, even with gradient clipping.

\textit{(3) SGGF is the best compromise.} The proposed Gated model (24.31~dB) and GatedLarge (24.48~dB) rank second and fourth on SD7K while maintaining training stability on both datasets. SGGF also achieves the highest TextPSNR (27.75~dB), suggesting superior text preservation.

\textit{(4) Scaling helps but has diminishing returns.} Large (dim=64, concat) at 24.51~dB nearly matches GatedLarge (24.48~dB) without any gating mechanism, indicating that increased capacity can partially compensate for simpler fusion. However, Gated (dim=48) at 24.31~dB outperforms Large on TextPSNR (27.75 vs.~27.61), suggesting gating is more text-aware.

\textit{(6) Significant improvement over prior SOTA.} We train the official DocDeshadower~\cite{li2023single} codebase on SD7K under identical conditions (200 epochs, same data augmentation). DocDeshadower achieves 21.81~dB PSNR---substantially below all our shadow-guided Restormer variants. This +2.96~dB gap (SGGF GatedLarge: 24.48~dB) stems from the backbone upgrade (CNN$\rightarrow$Transformer) and SGGF's effective cross-modal fusion, confirming that our Restormer-based framework sets a new state of the art.

\textit{(5) Cross-attention underperforms on Transformers.} SGCF (23.76~dB) barely improves over No SGCA (23.74~dB), yielding only +0.02~dB despite 4$\times$ higher memory consumption.
To understand why, we note that the Transformer decoder already fuses encoder features (via skip connections) through self-attention layers, which can learn to attend to shadow-relevant channels without explicit cross-attention. Adding cross-attention on top introduces redundant parameters that compete with---rather than complement---the self-attention mechanism.
The zero-initialized gate ($\gamma=0$) may also slow shadow feature integration during early training.
Evidence from the cross-dataset experiment (Sec.~\ref{sec:cross_dataset}) further supports this: SGCF's domain transfer (RDD$\rightarrow$SD7K: 14.60~dB) is no better than the Large model (14.72~dB), indicating that cross-attention does not learn more transferable representations.

\subsection{Training Stability Analysis}
\label{sec:stability}

A key finding is the divergence in training behavior between SD7K and RDD for the FiLM model.
Fig.~\ref{fig:stability} shows the training PSNR curves for all models on SD7K and the FiLM collapse on RDD.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig3_training_stability.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Training stability analysis.}
(a) On SD7K, all five fusion models converge stably over 200 epochs, with SGFM (FiLM) achieving the highest PSNR (24.77~dB) and SGGF reaching 24.31~dB.
(b) On RDD, SGFM (FiLM) training collapses to NaN at epoch~20 due to $\tanh$ gradient saturation, while SGGF maintains stable training throughout.}
\label{fig:stability}
\end{figure}

On SD7K, SGFM trains smoothly for all 200 epochs, achieving the best final performance (24.77~dB).

On RDD, SGFM without gradient clipping diverged at epochs 130 and 125 in two independent runs, producing NaN gradients.
We diagnose the root cause through gradient statistics (Fig.~\ref{fig:film_analysis}):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{$\tanh$ saturation dynamics}: When the FiLM scale factor saturates at $0$ or $2$, its derivative approaches zero. Unlike ReLU-based networks where some paths always have non-zero gradients, $\tanh$ saturation affects \textit{all} paths through the modulated features simultaneously, creating a ``dead zone'' from which the optimizer cannot escape.
  \item \textbf{Domain-dependent activation regime}: On SD7K, shadow patterns are synthetic and regular; the network learns modulation values within $\tanh$'s non-saturating range. On RDD, irregular real-world shadows demand larger modulation values that push $\tanh$ into saturation, triggering cascading gradient vanishing.
  \item \textbf{Gradient clipping as remedy}: With gradient clipping (max\_norm=1.0), SGFM~v2 trains stably on RDD, achieving 37.01~dB with the best TextPSNR (33.89). This confirms the failure mode is \textit{optimization-level} (unconstrained gradient flow through saturating nonlinearity) rather than \textit{architectural} (FiLM itself is not inherently unstable).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{SGGF's deliberate design.} Motivated by this diagnosis, SGGF uses sigmoid gates bounded in $(0,1)$ with a convex combination that guarantees numerical stability: $\mathbf{F}_{\text{out}}$ is always a weighted average of two bounded inputs, preventing the runaway activation values that precede FiLM's NaN collapse.

Two striking patterns emerge:

\textbf{(1) Asymmetric domain gap.} RDD$\rightarrow$SD7K achieves only $\sim$14.6~dB, near the level of random predictions, while SD7K$\rightarrow$RDD reaches $\sim$18.6~dB---a $\sim$4~dB gap that reveals fundamentally different transferability.

This finding carries a practical implication: \textbf{when training data is primarily synthetic but deployment is on real documents, the simplest fusion strategy (concatenation) is the safest choice}.

\subsection{Extended Training Dynamics}
\label{sec:extended}

To test whether our 200-epoch protocol provides sufficient convergence, we extend training of the top three SD7K models to 400 epochs.
All three exhibit \textbf{mild overfitting}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item SGGF Gated: 24.31 (E200) $\rightarrow$ 24.18 (E400), -0.13~dB
  \item SGGF GatedLarge: 24.48 (E200) $\rightarrow$ 24.29 (E400), -0.19~dB
  \item SGFM FiLM: 24.77 (E200) $\rightarrow$ 24.63 (E400), -0.14~dB
\end{itemize}

The consistent performance decline after 200 epochs---all three models, regardless of fusion type---suggests that 200 epochs is the optimal stopping point for SD7K-scale data.
This overfitting pattern further supports our Finding~2: larger models overfit more severely on synthetic data, as they have greater capacity to memorize dataset-specific artifacts rather than learning transferable shadow removal.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Evidence-Based Guidelines for Practitioners}

Our diagnostic study yields actionable, evidence-based recommendations:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Synthetic-only benchmarks are insufficient.} FiLM leads SD7K by a clear margin (24.77~dB) but collapses on RDD. Evaluating fusion strategies on synthetic data alone produces misleading rankings. We recommend that future work on document shadow removal always include at least one real-world dataset.
  \item \textbf{Simple concatenation is the best generalizer.} When training data is synthetic but deployment targets real documents, concatenation (No SGCA) achieves the best cross-domain transfer (18.59~dB SD7K$\rightarrow$RDD) with minimal overhead. This is the safest default for practitioners who cannot extensively tune per-dataset.
  \item \textbf{FiLM with gradient clipping is the best performer (if managed).} With gradient clipping, SGFM~v2 achieves 37.01~dB on RDD with the best TextPSNR (33.89). The key insight is that FiLM's optimization fragility can be entirely mitigated through gradient management---a simple fix that preserves its expressiveness advantage.
  \item \textbf{SGGF offers the best TextPSNR--stability trade-off.} Across both datasets, SGGF achieves the highest TextPSNR (27.75~dB on SD7K) and 100.2\% OCR recovery, with zero stability issues. For applications where text preservation is the primary goal, SGGF is the recommended choice.
  \item \textbf{Larger models are not always better.} Large (dim=64) models underperform their dim=48 counterparts on cross-domain transfer and show stronger overfitting at extended training (400 epochs). Increased capacity amplifies synthetic-domain overfitting rather than learning more generalizable features.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{Single-backbone scope.} Our study uses Restormer as the backbone to isolate fusion effects. While Restormer is representative of multi-scale Transformer architectures, extending this diagnostic framework to other backbones (e.g., SwinIR, Uformer) would test the generality of our findings.
One testable hypothesis emerging from our results is that \textbf{any Transformer backbone with self-attention over skip connections will exhibit similar fusion redundancy for cross-attention}, since the underlying mechanism (self-attention already performing cross-modal interaction) is architecture-agnostic.

\textbf{Text-aware optimization.} While our models improve OCR recovery rates, the loss function does not explicitly optimize for text legibility.
A text-aware perceptual loss, or an OCR-derived reward signal in a reinforcement learning framework, could further improve downstream task performance beyond what generic image quality metrics can achieve.

\textbf{Broader cross-modal restoration.} The fusion strategies and diagnostic methodology studied here are not specific to shadow removal.
The same question---\textit{how to optimally fuse an auxiliary modality into a Transformer restoration backbone}---arises in low-light enhancement (with exposure priors), deblurring (with blur kernel estimates), and deraining (with rain streak maps).
We encourage future work to replicate this diagnostic approach in other guided restoration tasks to test whether our findings (FiLM's synthetic-real dichotomy, concatenation's generalization advantage, cross-attention's Transformer redundancy) generalize across modalities.

\textbf{Theoretical understanding of FiLM instability.} While we empirically demonstrate that gradient clipping rescues FiLM training, a rigorous theoretical characterization of \textit{when} $\tanh$-based modulation becomes unstable remains open.
Analyzing the eigenvalue spectrum of the Fisher information matrix during FiLM training could reveal early-warning signals preceding NaN divergence, enabling proactive intervention rather than post-hoc gradient clipping.

% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================================

This paper presented the first systematic diagnosis of Transformer shadow fusion strategies for document image restoration.
Rather than proposing yet another fusion module in isolation, we asked a more fundamental question: \textbf{when and why} do different fusion mechanisms succeed or fail?

Our controlled study of five fusion strategies under identical training conditions revealed three findings that reshape our understanding of multi-modal fusion in document restoration:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The synthetic-real dichotomy}: FiLM achieves the best synthetic performance (+1.34~dB over Restormer on SD7K) but catastrophically collapses on real data due to $\tanh$ saturation---a failure mode fully rescued by gradient clipping, revealing an optimization-level rather than architectural root cause.
  \item \textbf{The asymmetric domain gap}: Cross-dataset evaluation reveals a $\sim$4~dB transferability gap (RDD$\rightarrow$SD7K: 14.6~dB vs. SD7K$\rightarrow$RDD: 18.6~dB), with simple concatenation emerging as the best cross-domain generalizer---a finding that challenges the common practice of using increasingly complex fusion mechanisms.
  \item \textbf{Evidence-based SGGF design}: Derived from the above diagnostic insights, SGGF's sigmoid-gated convex combination achieves the best TextPSNR (27.75~dB) and OCR recovery (100.2\%) with minimal overhead (+0.3M parameters, $<$1ms), surpassing the prior SOTA DocDeshadower by +2.96~dB on SD7K.
\end{enumerate}

Beyond these specific findings, our study demonstrates the value of \textbf{diagnostic evaluation over benchmark reporting}: by probing \textit{why} methods fail (gradient analysis, cross-domain transfer, ablation) rather than merely reporting \textit{what} scores they achieve, we uncover generalizable principles that can guide future multi-modal restoration research.
